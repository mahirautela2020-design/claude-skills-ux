# Leadership, Mentoring & Influence

## Purpose

This skill helps senior and lead designers expand their impact beyond individual projects into team, product, and organizational leadership.[web:407][web:409][web:410][web:411][web:412] It focuses on mentoring, shaping standards and quality bars, influencing product direction and cross‑team initiatives, and giving/receiving feedback while representing design credibly in strategic forums and aligning with product, engineering, and metrics practices from related skills.[web:407][web:409][web:412][web:418][web:417]

Use this skill when operating in senior/lead tracks, leveling up your influence, or supporting the growth of other designers.

---

## Instructions

### Step 1: Clarify your leadership scope and responsibilities

Define what **leadership** means in your current role.[web:409][web:411][web:412]

Ask:

- "What decisions do I influence today (team, product area, org)?"
- "Where am I expected to lead: craft, people, process, or strategy?"
- "What impact am I accountable for beyond my own delivery?"

Summarize:

- Primary leadership scopes (e.g., own a domain, mentor designers, drive a cross‑team initiative, steward a design system).
- Key partners (PMs, Eng leads, other design leads) you must influence and support.[web:409][web:412]

Use this to focus your energy on the areas where your leadership can most improve outcomes, not just artifacts.

---

### Step 2: Practice intentional mentoring

Treat **mentoring** as a structured, ongoing practice, not ad‑hoc advice.[web:408][web:413][web:416][web:419]

For each mentee:

- Align on goals:
  - Skills they want to grow.
  - Types of support they need (feedback, shadowing, sponsorship).
- Set a simple structure:
  - Regular cadence (e.g., bi‑weekly).
  - Shared doc or board for topics, actions, and progress.
- Mix:
  - Craft feedback (e.g., on current work).
  - Process and stakeholder coaching.
  - Career and growth conversations.

Encourage mentees to own the agenda and come with questions; your role is to guide, challenge, and unblock, not to solve everything for them.[web:416][web:413][web:408]

---

### Step 3: Shape standards and quality bars

Help define and uphold **quality standards** for design work.[web:412][web:418][web:421]

Practices:

- Co‑create and refine:
  - Design principles.
  - Definition of done for UX (states covered, accessibility, error flows, etc.).
  - Examples of “good” for key artefacts (flows, specs, research reports, interaction patterns).[web:421]
- Use critiques and reviews to:
  - Tie feedback to standards and principles, not personal taste.
  - Highlight positive examples as well as gaps.

Document standards in a visible place (e.g., craft excellence guidelines, pattern library) and reinforce them in hiring, onboarding, and performance conversations.[web:412][web:421][web:418]

---

### Step 4: Influence product direction and cross‑team initiatives

Move from **designing features** to helping shape **what** the team works on.[web:409][web:410][web:411]

Practices:

- Partner with PMs to:
  - Frame and prioritize problems and opportunities.
  - Bring user insights into roadmap and strategy discussions.
- Identify cross‑team initiatives:
  - Common UX problems (e.g., onboarding, navigation, trust) that span squads.
  - Horizontal work like design systems, accessibility, experimentation, or research practices.[web:409][web:412]
- Lead or co‑lead these initiatives:
  - Define vision and success criteria.
  - Align multiple teams around shared frameworks, patterns, or journeys.

Use data and stories to connect design proposals to business outcomes and strategic bets, leveraging `product-ux-strategy-alignment`, `product-discovery-opportunity`, and `ux-product-design-metrics`.[web:409][web:410][web:411]

---

### Step 5: Represent design in strategic forums

Show up as a **credible, prepared voice** for users and design in leadership settings.[web:409][web:410][web:412]

When joining roadmap reviews, planning, or leadership meetings:

- Come prepared with:
  - Clear articulation of user problems and opportunities.
  - Evidence from research and experiments.
  - Simple visuals (journeys, OSTs, before/after examples) when helpful.
- Speak in terms of:
  - Outcomes and trade‑offs.
  - Risk and opportunity, not just UX polish.

Aim to be seen as a **trusted advisor**, not merely a service provider: someone who helps the group make better decisions and manages change effectively.[web:409][web:410][web:411]

---

### Step 6: Give feedback that grows people and work

As a leader, your feedback should improve both **outcomes** and **confidence**.[web:412][web:418]

Guidelines:

- Be specific and behavioural:
  - Point to concrete examples in the work.
  - Describe impact (“This may confuse users because…”).
- Balance:
  - Appreciation (what’s working well).
  - Suggestions (where to explore further).
- Focus on:
  - Alignment with problem, users, and strategy.
  - Risks and trade‑offs rather than purely aesthetic preferences.

Adjust tone for experience level: more directive for juniors, more coaching and questioning for seniors. Use team standards as the reference point, not personal style.[web:412][web:418][web:421]

---

### Step 7: Receive feedback and model growth

Model **healthy feedback behaviour** by how you receive it.[web:411][web:417]

Practices:

- Proactively ask for feedback from:
  - Peers.
  - PM and engineering partners.
  - Your own reports.
- Listen fully before responding; clarify, don’t defend.
- Reflect and act:
  - Decide what you will change.
  - Share back how you applied feedback, when appropriate.

This builds trust and shows that feedback is a normal part of growth at every level, countering “leadership mediocrity” and keeping standards high.[web:415][web:418][web:417]

---

### Step 8: Build and nurture design culture

Leaders shape the **culture** of the design team.[web:407][web:412][web:418]

Examples:

- Encourage:
  - Sharing work early and often.
  - Curiosity about users and other disciplines.
  - Psychological safety in critiques and retros.
- Support:
  - Reasonable workloads and healthy boundaries.
  - Recognition of contributions, not just outcomes.
- Embed design thinking beyond the team:
  - Invite cross‑functional partners into research and workshops.
  - Share stories and frameworks that help others think in user‑centred ways.

Culture is reinforced by repeated behaviours; be intentional about the habits you model and reward, and use `collab-design-ops-process` to encode them into rituals and processes.

---

### Step 9: Grow the next generation of leaders

Use your position to **create more leaders**, not just more followers.[web:407][web:412][web:416]

Practices:

- Delegate ownership:
  - Have others lead parts of projects, workshops, or initiatives.
  - Support them publicly, coach them privately.
- Expose emerging leaders to:
  - Strategy and stakeholder meetings.
  - Cross‑team collaboration opportunities.
- Help them:
  - Articulate their own point of view.
  - Build networks beyond the design team.

Track their growth over time with tools like skills/competency matrices and mentoring programs, and adjust responsibilities so they can stretch safely.[web:393][web:404][web:416][web:419]

## Examples

### Example 1: Senior designer shaping a cross-team initiative

- Situation:
  - Multiple teams created inconsistent onboarding experiences.
- Leadership:
  - Senior designer led a cross‑team effort to define shared principles and patterns.
  - Partnered with PMs and Eng leads to align on metrics and roadmap impact.
- Outcomes:
  - Coherent onboarding across products, clearer activation metrics, and a stronger perception of design as a strategic partner.[web:409][web:410][web:412]

### Example 2: Mentoring and raising quality bars

- Situation:
  - Varied quality across design work; juniors unsure what “good” looks like.
- Leadership:
  - Lead designer created a quality checklist and example gallery.
  - Set up regular mentoring and crits focused on applying these standards.
- Outcomes:
  - More consistent UX across products and faster growth for juniors, with clearer expectations and feedback loops.[web:412][web:418][web:421][web:416]

## Troubleshooting

### You feel stuck in execution, not influence

- Spend more time upstream:
  - Join discovery and strategy discussions.
  - Bring user insights and opportunity framing to PMs and leaders.[web:409][web:411]
- Take ownership of at least one cross‑team or strategic initiative, even if small.

### Mentoring feels ad‑hoc or ineffective

- Align expectations and goals with mentees explicitly.
- Add a simple structure (cadence, shared doc, recurring topics).
- Ask mentees regularly what’s helping and what they’d like more/less of.[web:413][web:416][web:419]

### Feedback culture is weak or tense

- Model the behaviour you want:
  - Ask for feedback, receive it gracefully, and act on it.
  - Give feedback that is specific, kind, and focused on growth.[web:412][web:418]
- Use team rituals (crits, retros) to normalize constructive feedback and learning, working with `collab-design-ops-process`.

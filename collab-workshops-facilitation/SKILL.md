# Workshop Design & Facilitation

## Purpose

This skill helps plan and run UX and product workshops that actually produce decisions and shared understanding, rather than unfocused meetings.[web:173][web:174][web:171][web:175][web:179][web:183] It covers designing discovery, ideation, prioritization, and alignment sessions, and using collaborative tools (FigJam, Miro) and methods (Crazy 8s, mapping, prioritization frameworks) effectively, connected to opportunity framing, strategy, and research follow‑ups from related skills.[web:176][web:180][web:184][web:177][web:185][web:178][web:182]

Use this skill when you want to bring a cross‑functional group together to define problems, generate ideas, prioritize work, or build alignment on direction.

---

## Instructions

### Step 1: Define purpose, outcomes, and participants

Start by making the workshop **purposeful and scoped**.[web:173][web:174][web:175][web:179][web:183]

Ask:

- "What decision or outcome should this workshop produce (not just activities)?"
- "Which part of the process is this: discovery, ideation, prioritization, alignment, or critique?"
- "Who needs to be in the room to make decisions and provide key perspectives?"

Define:

- A short purpose statement (e.g., “Align on top 3 onboarding problems to tackle next quarter”).
- 1–3 concrete outputs (e.g., prioritized opportunity list, mapped journey, 10–20 sketched ideas).
- A participant list representing design, PM, engineering, and relevant business stakeholders.[web:173][web:175][web:183]

Where relevant, tie the purpose to outcomes or opportunity spaces defined in `product-discovery-opportunity`, `product-ux-strategy-alignment`, or `ux-mindset-problem-framing`.

---

### Step 2: Choose workshop type and core activities

Select the **type of workshop** and supporting activities based on your goal.[web:173][web:175][web:171][web:183]

Examples:

- **Discovery / empathy**:
  - Activities: stakeholder interviews, journey mapping, “What we know / think we know / don’t know” boards.
- **Ideation / design**:
  - Activities: Crazy 8s, “How Might We” clustering, sketching and sharing, concept posters.[web:176][web:180][web:184][web:171]
- **Prioritization**:
  - Activities: impact/effort matrix, MoSCoW, dot voting, RICE card sorting.[web:177][web:179][web:183][web:185]
- **Alignment / critique**:
  - Activities: design walkthroughs, structured critique (e.g., “I like / I wish / What if”), decision mapping.[web:173][web:183]

Pick 2–4 activities that fit your time box (e.g., 1.5–3 hours) and purpose, and that you can clearly connect to next steps (e.g., experiments, roadmap items, research).

---

### Step 3: Design the agenda and flow

Create a **time‑boxed agenda** that balances divergence and convergence.[web:171][web:175][web:179][web:183]

Typical structure:

1. **Opening (10–15 min)**:
   - Welcome, purpose, outcomes, agenda, roles.
   - Ground rules (e.g., one conversation at a time, critique ideas not people).
2. **Warm‑up (5–10 min)**:
   - Quick exercise to get people talking and comfortable with the tool.
3. **Core activities (60–120 min)**:
   - Discovery/mapping, then ideation, then prioritization, depending on goal.
4. **Decisions & next steps (15–30 min)**:
   - Summarize key outputs, decisions, owners, and follow‑up actions.

Assign rough time to each segment and plan buffer for discussion. Ensure the agenda explicitly ladders up to strategic outcomes or opportunity questions where possible.

---

### Step 4: Set up collaborative boards and materials

Prepare the **workspace** in FigJam, Miro, or similar.[web:178][web:182][web:171][web:179]

Include:

- Clear sections for each agenda step, with labels and brief instructions.
- Pre‑made templates:
  - Journey maps or service blueprints.
  - Crazy 8s frames.
  - Impact/effort grids or other prioritization matrices.
  - Stickies and dot voting markers.
- Example cards for the first few items to model what “good” looks like.

Share access and any pre‑reads in advance so participants can log in and orient themselves.

---

### Step 5: Facilitate discovery and mapping activities

For **discovery, empathy, or alignment** workshops:[web:173][web:175][web:171][web:179][web:183]

- Use activities like:
  - “What we know / think we know / don’t know” about users and problems.
  - Journey mapping or service mapping for key flows.
  - Affinity clustering of existing insights or pain points.
- As facilitator:
  - Keep the focus on user stories and evidence, not solutions.
  - Capture pain points, questions, and assumptions visually.
  - Summarize themes periodically to keep everyone aligned.

Aim to leave with a clearer shared picture of the problem space and user context, ready to feed into `product-discovery-opportunity` and `ux-mindset-problem-framing`.

---

### Step 6: Facilitate ideation: Crazy 8s and beyond

For **ideation** workshops, use methods that encourage broad idea generation.[web:176][web:180][web:184]

Typical flow:

- Present a clear problem statement or “How Might We” question.
- Run **Crazy 8s**:
  - Each participant sketches 8 ideas in 8 minutes for the same problem.[web:176][web:180][web:184]
  - Emphasize quantity and diversity over polish.
- Have participants briefly share their favourite 1–2 ideas.
- Cluster ideas into themes and label them.

You can follow up with:

- Dot voting to identify concepts worth exploring.
- Small groups refining selected ideas into more detailed concepts or flows.[web:171][web:175][web:183]

Keep the energy high and ensure quieter voices have space to contribute. Capture the strongest ideas so they can move into prototyping and validation (`ux-prototyping-validation`).

---

### Step 7: Run prioritization and decision activities

When there are too many ideas or opportunities, facilitate **prioritization**.[web:177][web:179][web:183][web:185]

Methods:

- **Impact/Effort matrix**:
  - Place ideas based on user/business impact and implementation effort.[web:177][web:183][web:185]
  - “Quick wins” (high impact/low effort) often become near‑term focus.
- **MoSCoW** (Must, Should, Could, Won’t):
  - Categorize ideas or features to clarify scope for a release or timeframe.[web:177][web:181][web:185]
- **Dot voting**:
  - Give participants a limited number of votes to highlight what they believe matters most.[web:171][web:179][web:183]
- **RICE or similar frameworks** (if your team uses them):
  - Rough scoring by reach, impact, confidence, and effort.[web:177][web:181][web:185]

Ensure final outputs include a **short list of prioritized items**, not just raw votes, and align them with product/UX strategy and opportunity sizing where possible (`product-discovery-opportunity`, `product-ux-strategy-alignment`).

---

### Step 8: Facilitate remote collaboration effectively

For remote or hybrid workshops, pay extra attention to **participation and pacing**.[web:178][web:182][web:171][web:183]

Tips:

- Use built‑in facilitation tools:
  - Timers for activities.
  - Voting tools for prioritization.
  - Cursors or avatars to see who is working where.[web:182][web:178]
- Mix individual and group work:
  - Solo sketching or note writing to avoid groupthink.
  - Group discussion and clustering after individual work.[web:171][web:175]
- Encourage participation:
  - Call on different people to share.
  - Use chat or reactions for those less comfortable speaking.

Plan short breaks for longer workshops to avoid fatigue.

---

### Step 9: Close with decisions, owners, and follow‑up

End the workshop by making **outcomes and next steps explicit**.[web:173][web:179][web:183]

Summarize:

- What you produced:
  - Mapped journeys, themes, ideas, prioritized lists.
- What was decided:
  - Which problems or ideas move forward, and in what form.
- Who owns what:
  - Named owners for follow‑up actions (e.g., refine concepts, validate with users, prepare roadmap items or experiments).
- When:
  - Timelines for next steps and check‑ins.

After the session:

- Share a short recap:
  - Screenshots or links to boards.
  - Key decisions and action items.
  - Any open questions that need additional work.

This turns workshop energy into concrete progress rather than a one‑off event and connects outputs into research, experimentation, and roadmapping streams.

## Examples

### Example 1: Discovery and alignment workshop

- Purpose:
  - Align on top user problems in onboarding.
- Activities:
  - “What we know / think we know / don’t know”.
  - Journey mapping for first‑time users.
  - Thematic clustering of pain points.[web:173][web:171][web:175]
- Outcome:
  - Shared journey map, top 3 problem themes, list of assumptions to validate next via `ux-user-research` and `product-discovery-opportunity`.

### Example 2: Ideation and prioritization workshop

- Purpose:
  - Generate and prioritize ideas to improve dashboard usability.
- Activities:
  - Problem recap and “How Might We” questions.
  - Crazy 8s sketching in FigJam.
  - Dot voting and Impact/Effort matrix.[web:176][web:180][web:184][web:177][web:183]
- Outcome:
  - 40+ idea sketches, 5 promising concepts, 3 chosen for prototyping and user testing (`ux-prototyping-validation`), with a short list mapped to product outcomes.

## Troubleshooting

### Workshops drift off-topic or run out of time

- Reiterate purpose and outcomes at the start of each segment.
- Gently park off-topic items in a “parking lot” for later.
- Use timers and stick to time boxes; be willing to cut or compress activities.[web:173][web:174][web:175][web:171][web:183]

### A few voices dominate

- Use individual exercises (Crazy 8s, silent dot voting) to balance input.[web:171][web:176][web:180][web:184]
- Invite quieter participants to share explicitly.
- Set norms about equal participation up front.

### Outcomes feel vague or not actionable

- Reserve time at the end for explicit decision making and action planning.
- Translate outputs into a small set of prioritized items with owners and links to strategy or opportunity spaces.
- Follow up quickly with a written summary and next steps.

# Design Operations & Process

## Purpose

This skill helps designers strengthen team operations: shared rituals, templates, documentation, and skills management that make UX work more effective and sustainable.[web:210][web:211][web:205][web:207] It also covers maintaining a skills matrix, mentoring others, and continuously improving ways of working through retrospectives and small process changes, in alignment with product and engineering practices, and outcome/metrics thinking from related skills.[web:204][web:214][web:206][web:209][web:201][web:208][web:400][web:403]

Use this skill when you’re shaping how the design team collaborates, not just the design of individual products.

---

## Instructions

### Step 1: Clarify team goals and pain points

Start by understanding what **design operations** should improve.[web:205][web:210][web:211][web:203]

Ask the team:

- "What slows us down or creates rework?"
- "Where do handoffs, communication, or alignment break down?"
- "Which rituals or processes already work well that we should keep?"

Summarize:

- 3–5 key pain points (e.g., unclear handoffs, inconsistent critique, poor documentation).
- 2–3 desired outcomes (e.g., smoother releases, better alignment, less burnout).[web:207][web:205][web:210]

Use these as anchors for any process or ritual changes, and link them to product/UX outcomes where possible (`product-ux-strategy-alignment`).

---

### Step 2: Strengthen core design rituals

Support and improve **regular design rituals** such as crits, design reviews, and share-outs.[web:210][web:211][web:205]

For each ritual:

- Clarify purpose:
  - Crits: improve work through peer feedback.
  - Reviews: align with product/engineering, make decisions.
  - Show & tell: share learnings and patterns.
- Define cadence, participants, and roles (facilitator, presenter, note-taker).
- Create lightweight templates for:
  - Agenda.
  - What to present (problem, context, work, feedback needed).
  - How to capture decisions and actions.[web:210][web:211][web:205]

Ensure rituals stay focused, time-boxed, and useful; adjust based on feedback and retrospectives.

---

### Step 3: Run and improve retrospectives

Use **retrospectives** regularly to improve the team’s process.[web:204][web:201][web:208][web:400][web:406][web:403]

For a project or time period:

- Ask:
  - “What went well?”
  - “What was challenging or frustrating?”
  - “What should we try differently next time?”
- Use simple formats:
  - Start / Stop / Continue.
  - Mad / Sad / Glad (for team morale and collaboration).
  - “Puzzles / Risks / Appreciations”.

Agree on a small number of **action items** with owners and due dates.[web:204][web:208]

Follow up in future retros to check which improvements stuck and whether they helped, treating retros as part of iterative design and delivery rather than one-off meetings.[web:400][web:406]

---

### Step 4: Create and maintain shared templates

Develop **templates** that make recurring work easier and more consistent.[web:202][web:205][web:210]

Common templates include:

- Research plans and reports.
- Problem framing and opportunity briefs.
- Design specs and handoff checklists.
- Workshop agendas and boards.
- Retro boards.

For each template:

- Clarify when to use it.
- Keep it as simple as possible while capturing essential information.
- Store it in a central, easy-to-find place (design system site, Notion, intranet).[web:202][web:205][web:210]

Review templates periodically to remove outdated ones and improve based on team feedback and evolving practices.

---

### Step 5: Improve documentation and decision tracking

Turn **decisions and patterns** into living documentation.[web:205][web:210][web:212]

Practices:

- Document key design decisions and rationale in a shared place.
- Capture patterns and guidelines:
  - Component usage rules.
  - Interaction patterns.
  - Accessibility and content guidelines.
- Link documentation from:
  - Design system.
  - Project docs.
  - Tickets or epics.

Aim for documentation that is “just enough” to be useful and kept up to date, rather than perfect but stale.[web:205][web:210][web:212]

---

### Step 6: Maintain and use a skills matrix

Use a **skills matrix** to understand and grow team capabilities.[web:214][web:206][web:209][web:392][web:393][web:401]

Steps:

- Define skills relevant to your team (e.g., research, IA, interaction, visual design, product thinking, facilitation, Ops).
- For each skill, define levels or descriptors (e.g., awareness, working, advanced, expert).
- Ask team members to self-assess and, where appropriate, have managers or peers provide input.

Use the matrix to:

- Identify strengths and gaps at team level.
- Plan learning and pairing/mentoring opportunities.
- Inform staffing decisions for projects and initiatives.[web:206][web:209][web:214][web:401]

Frame the matrix as a **development** tool, not a performance rating system, to encourage honest self-assessment and growth.[web:401][web:404][web:405]

---

### Step 7: Support mentoring and knowledge sharing

Use the skills matrix and rituals to **enable mentoring** and peer learning.[web:206][web:209][web:207][web:398]

Practices:

- Pair less-experienced designers with more-experienced ones on relevant skills.
- Set up:
  - Regular 1:1 mentoring or peer coaching.
  - Brown-bag sessions or internal talks where team members share case studies or methods.
- Encourage documentation of learnings from projects and experiments.

This turns the team’s experience into a shared asset, not just individual knowledge, and supports structured growth paths.[web:398][web:404][web:401]

---

### Step 8: Align design processes with product and engineering

Ensure design processes fit into **broader product and engineering workflows**.[web:210][web:205][web:188][web:212]

Examples:

- Clarify where design activities fit within:
  - Discovery and validation.
  - Backlog refinement.
  - Sprint planning.
- Define expected outputs at each stage:
  - Discovery insights, problem briefs, prototypes, specs.
- Coordinate with Product and Eng Ops (if present) to:
  - Align cadences (e.g., sprint length, planning cycles).
  - Integrate design reviews and UX checks into broader delivery processes.[web:210][web:212][web:205]

Update process diagrams and checklists when practices evolve, and connect them to strategy/metrics work so process supports outcome-driven design.

---

### Step 9: Protect focus and sustainable ways of working

Use Design Ops to **protect focus time and team health**.[web:207][web:203][web:210][web:211]

Practices:

- Limit unnecessary meetings; cluster rituals into predictable blocks.
- Encourage blocking focus time in calendars for deep work.
- Use retros and 1:1s to surface workload and burnout risks early.[web:207][web:203][web:398]
- Automate or streamline repetitive tasks where possible (e.g., using tooling for specs, tokens, and visual regression).[web:205][web:210]

The goal is to create a sustainable environment where quality UX work can thrive.

## Examples

### Example 1: Improving design reviews and handoff

- Pain points:
  - Inconsistent design reviews, late feedback, messy handoffs.
- Ops changes:
  - Introduced a weekly crit with a clear agenda.
  - Created a standard design spec template and Figma file structure.
  - Added a short pre-release UX review step.[web:205][web:210][web:211]
- Outcome:
  - Fewer last-minute surprises, smoother collaboration, and reduced rework.

### Example 2: Skills matrix and mentoring

- Situation:
  - Team unsure where strengths and gaps were; junior designers needed guidance.
- Ops changes:
  - Defined a skills matrix with core UX and product skills.
  - Ran a self-assessment and used results to match mentors and mentees.
  - Planned learning sessions around common gaps.[web:214][web:206][web:209][web:401]
- Outcome:
  - Clearer growth paths for designers and better distribution of expertise.

## Troubleshooting

### Rituals feel like “meetings for meetings’ sake”

- Check whether each ritual has a clear purpose and outcome.
- Reduce frequency or length where they aren’t adding value.
- In retros, ask which rituals help and which need redesign or removal.[web:204][web:211][web:203][web:400]

### Documentation becomes outdated or ignored

- Keep docs lightweight and close to where work happens (e.g., linked from design files and tickets).
- Assign ownership for key docs (e.g., design system, process guides).
- Use retros to identify which docs are helpful and which can be archived.[web:205][web:210][web:212]

### Skills matrix feels like a performance rating tool

- Frame it as a **development** tool, not evaluation.
- Emphasize self-awareness and growth, not grades.
- Use it to create opportunities (pairing, learning), not to judge people.[web:206][web:209][web:214][web:401]

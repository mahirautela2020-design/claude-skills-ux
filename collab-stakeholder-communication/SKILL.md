# Stakeholder Management & Communication

## Purpose

This skill helps designers communicate their work clearly, build trust with stakeholders, and reduce rework by using structured storytelling, documented rationale, and intentional feedback loops.[web:377][web:383][web:390] It also guides how to adapt communication for different audiences such as PMs, engineers, executives, and non-design stakeholders, while tying stories back to strategy, opportunities, and metrics from `product-ux-strategy-alignment`, `product-discovery-opportunity`, and `ux-product-design-metrics`.[web:378][web:384][web:382]

Use this skill when preparing design reviews, sharing research or design decisions, or working through feedback with cross-functional partners.

---

## Instructions

### Step 1: Clarify audience and objective

Before preparing any communication, clarify **who you’re speaking to** and **what you need from them**.[web:390]

Ask:

- "Who will be in the room (PMs, engineers, execs, marketing, support)?"
- "What is the main objective (inform, decide, align, get approval, gather feedback)?"
- "What decisions or actions do I need from this group by the end of the meeting or message?"[web:377][web:382]

Summarize this in 1–2 lines; use it to focus your story, slides, or document and to scope which metrics, journeys, or opportunities you reference.

---

### Step 2: Structure the story: context → evidence → insight → recommendation

Use a simple storytelling structure instead of jumping straight into screens.[web:377][web:384][web:388]

A practical pattern:

1. **Context** – What problem, opportunity, or outcome is this work addressing (tie to strategy/metrics if possible)?
2. **Evidence** – What research, data, or stakeholder input informed your thinking?
3. **Insight** – What did you learn that matters for decisions?
4. **Recommendation / Design** – What are you proposing and why?
5. **Ask** – What decision or feedback do you need now?[web:380][web:382]

Example mini-script for each section you present:

- “Here’s the problem or outcome we’re focused on.”
- “Here’s what we heard or saw (evidence).”
- “This tells us [insight] about user needs or constraints.”
- “Here’s the design/change that responds to that insight.”
- “Feedback: I’d like input on X and Y, not colours or minor copy today.”[web:377][web:388]

This keeps stakeholders focused on user and business needs instead of personal preferences and makes it easy to plug into opportunity maps, OSTs, and metrics briefs from related skills.[web:384][web:390]

---

### Step 3: Document design rationale

Capture **why** decisions were made so you can explain and revisit them.[web:383][web:389]

For key decisions, note:

- Problem or constraint being solved.
- Options considered (including those not chosen).
- Evidence or principles used (research, heuristics, technical constraints, strategy).
- Final decision and reasoning.

You can keep this in:

- A rationale section in your design doc.
- Comments or notes in your design tool.
- A short “Decision log” for larger projects.

This reduces repeated debates, supports handover, and helps new stakeholders catch up quickly.[web:383][web:389]

---

### Step 4: Plan and manage feedback loops

Treat feedback as a **structured loop**, not ad-hoc chaos.[web:383][web:386]

Before reviews:

- Set expectations:
  - Purpose of the review (exploratory, directional, final sign‑off).
  - What kind of feedback is useful now (e.g., problem framing vs micro‑visual details).
- Share pre‑reads when helpful so stakeholders can arrive prepared.

During reviews:

- Anchor feedback in evidence and goals:
  - “Does this concept solve the problem we defined?” vs “Do you like this colour?”
- Capture:
  - Feedback items.
  - Decisions made.
  - Open questions and owners.[web:383]

After reviews:

- Summarize decisions and next steps in writing and share with attendees.[web:383][web:389]
- Close the loop by showing how you addressed feedback in the next iteration (or why you did not).

---

### Step 5: Adapt communication by stakeholder type

Tailor depth and framing to different audiences.[web:378][web:390]

For **PMs**:

- Emphasize:
  - User problems, opportunities, and impact on product metrics.
  - Trade‑offs and constraints you considered.
- Keep it collaborative:
  - Invite co‑ownership of decisions and next experiments.

For **engineers**:

- Emphasize:
  - Clear requirements, edge cases, and behaviour.
  - Rationale for complexity vs simplicity.
- Be precise and concrete:
  - Provide annotated flows, specs, and examples.

For **executives / leadership**:

- Emphasize:
  - High-level narrative: problem, opportunity, impact on strategy and KPIs.[web:382][web:379]
- Use conclusion‑first logic:
  - Recommendation → impact → evidence → next steps.

For **non-design stakeholders** (e.g., marketing, sales, support):

- Emphasize:
  - How changes affect customers, messaging, and their workflows.
  - What will change in practice and when.[web:390]

Adjust detail based on time and audience; put deep detail in appendices or follow-ups.

---

### Step 6: Use simple storytelling frameworks in design reviews

Use lightweight frameworks to structure design presentations.[web:377][web:388]

Two practical patterns:

- **Problem → Agitate → Solution (PAS)**:
  - Problem: Describe the user/business problem with data or stories.
  - Agitate: Show consequences or impact if not addressed.
  - Solution: Present the design that addresses it and why it works.
- **Options & Analysis**:
  - Show 2–3 viable options for solving the problem.
  - Compare them along key criteria (user impact, feasibility, alignment).
  - Recommend one option and explain why, inviting discussion.[web:384][web:388]

Pick a pattern depending on whether you need **buy‑in for a clear recommendation** (PAS) or **co‑decision on trade‑offs** (Options & Analysis).

---

### Step 7: Handle disagreements and conflicting feedback

When stakeholders disagree:

- Return to shared goals:
  - “Which option better serves the agreed user outcome or metric?”
- Separate:
  - Facts (data, research).
  - Assumptions (what we think is true but haven’t validated).
  - Preferences (subjective likes/dislikes).[web:383][web:389]

If conflict persists:

- Propose:
  - A small test or research activity to get more evidence (`product-experimentation-data`, `ux-user-research`).
  - A time‑bound decision (e.g., pick option A now, revisit after learning X).
- Clarify decision owners so debates don’t remain open indefinitely.

---

### Step 8: Communicate progress and changes transparently

Keep stakeholders aligned over time, not just in big reviews.[web:383][web:390]

Practices:

- Share short updates:
  - What changed since last time.
  - What you learned (from research, testing, or experiments).
  - What’s next and any decisions needed.
- Use lightweight artefacts:
  - Before/after snapshots.
  - Journey or flow updates.
  - Lists of resolved vs open issues.

This builds trust and reduces surprises late in delivery.[web:383][web:386]

---

### Step 9: Build long-term relationships and trust

Stakeholder management is also about **relationships**, not just meetings.[web:380][web:384][web:390]

Behaviours:

- Listen actively and reflect back concerns to show understanding.
- Be transparent about trade‑offs and constraints; don’t oversell certainty.
- Acknowledge others’ expertise (engineering complexity, business constraints, customer knowledge).
- Follow through on commitments and close loops on feedback.

Over time, this makes it easier to advocate for UX decisions and get support for user‑centred choices.

## Examples

### Example 1: Design review with PM and engineering

- Audience & objective:
  - PM + engineering, goal is to align on direction and identify risks.
- Story:
  - Context: onboarding drop‑off problem tied to activation metrics.
  - Evidence: analytics + usability findings.
  - Insight: users don’t understand required steps; copy and structure confuse them.
  - Recommendation: simplified 4‑step flow with clearer progress and guidance.
- Communication:
  - Emphasized impact on activation and engineering complexity.
  - Captured decisions and follow‑ups in a shared doc and decision log.

### Example 2: Executive update on UX initiative

- Audience & objective:
  - Execs, goal is to show impact and secure support for next phase.
- Story:
  - Start with recommendation and impact (activation up, support tickets down).
  - Show key user stories and 2–3 metrics aligned to strategy.
  - Outline next UX bets tied to strategic goals.
- Communication:
  - High‑level visuals, minimal screens.
  - Clear ask: resources to extend the pattern to other flows.

## Troubleshooting

### Reviews devolve into opinion battles

- Re-anchor on problem, evidence, and goals before discussing solutions.[web:383][web:390]
- Ask: “What user or business evidence would help us decide between these options?”
- Use structured frameworks (PAS, Options & Analysis) to focus discussion.[web:377][web:388]

### Stakeholders feel lost or overwhelmed

- Simplify presentations:
  - Fewer slides, clearer narrative, conclusion first.[web:382][web:390]
- Tailor detail to audience; put deep details in appendices or follow‑ups.
- Explicitly state: “Today, I need us to decide X; the rest is context.”

### Feedback is vague or unhelpful

- Ask more specific prompts:
  - “How well does this address the problem we defined?”
  - “Where do you see risk for your team in this approach?”
- Encourage stakeholders to tie feedback to goals and constraints, not just taste.[web:383][web:386]
